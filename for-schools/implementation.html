For Schools & Districts

A K-12 framework for actually implementing AI literacy.

Strong content is not enough. Schools need a path from AI awareness to classroom integration to system-wide adoption — one that fits the constraints they already work inside. This is that path.

The problem this solves

Most schools want to do something about AI. Teachers are curious but uncertain. Leadership wants a strategy but lacks one. Parents have questions no one is answering well. Tools get adopted without coherence, and the result is fragmented — a few enthusiastic classrooms, a lot of confusion, and no shared understanding of what good AI literacy actually looks like.

AI in schools should not be a tool conversation. It should be a learning conversation. The framework below moves a school through the four levels where that conversation actually has to happen: the teacher, the classroom, the building, and the community around it.

The four phases

Phase 1

Teacher Readiness

Goal: Build teacher confidence and foundational AI literacy.

  • AESOP AI Literacy Course (teacher track). Self-paced foundation in how AI systems actually work, what they can and cannot do, and where the meaningful risks live.
  • AI for Teachers quick-start modules. Short, practical units on prompting for lesson design, AI-assisted differentiation, and ethical use in the classroom.
  • No technical background required. Built for classroom teachers, not engineers. The entry barrier is curiosity, not credentials.
  • Time-respecting. Modules are sized to fit inside planning periods, not to demand evenings and weekends.
Phase 2

Classroom Integration

Goal: Move from teacher knowledge to instructional practice.

  • Subject-specific integration examples. AI-assisted problem solving in math, feedback workflows in ELA, inquiry support in science and social studies.
  • Student-centered AI activities. Lessons where students use AI to investigate, not lessons where AI replaces investigation.
  • Standards-aligned lesson plans. Designed to extend existing curriculum, not compete with it.
  • Evaluation built in. Every activity includes how to tell whether students learned, not just whether they finished.
Phase 3

School-Wide Implementation

Goal: Build coherence and leadership alignment across the building.

  • AI integration roadmap. A sequenced plan from pilot classrooms to grade-level adoption to building-wide coherence.
  • Leadership guide for principals. What to ask, what to support, what to fund, and what to leave alone.
  • Curriculum and assessment alignment. AI use connected to the actual learning targets the school is already accountable for.
  • Equity-aware design. Implementation choices that close access gaps rather than widening them.
Phase 4

Systems & Community Alignment

Goal: Sustain implementation beyond a single year or champion teacher.

  • Parent education resources. Plain-language explainers families can actually use to understand what their kids are doing with AI and why.
  • AI policy templates. Starter policies for safe and responsible use — including the questions a school should answer before adopting any new tool.
  • Professional learning community model. Structures for teachers to learn from each other once the initial rollout is over.
  • Ongoing review. Practices and questions that keep the work honest as the technology and the evidence both change.

What this framework is — and isn't

What it is

  • A practical sequence schools can follow without rebuilding their curriculum.
  • Aligned to AESOP courses and the AESOP transcript system.
  • Designed to be piloted in 1–3 schools before scaling.
  • Compatible with existing standards work (ISTE, AI4K12, CSTA).
  • Free to use. The core curriculum stays free to learners.

What it isn't

  • A replacement for the curriculum a school already has.
  • A vendor sales funnel disguised as a framework.
  • A promise that AI can fix what was already broken.
  • A reason to skip the hard conversations about consent, bias, and surveillance.
  • A finished thing. It will change as schools push back on it.

Pilot opportunity

AESOP is identifying 1–3 partner schools to pilot this framework in the 2026–2027 school year. The pilot focuses on teacher onboarding, two-subject classroom integration, and a structured feedback loop to refine the framework before broader release. International pilot sites are also welcome, particularly where existing curriculum constraints make traditional EdTech adoption difficult.

If your school or district is interested, the conversation starts with a 45-minute discovery call. No commitment, no procurement process, no sales pressure — this is a working partnership, not a license.
